HOW LIGHT WORKS Visible light is actually a tiny slice of the electromagnetic spectrum. What we call "white light" is all the colors of the rainbow combined — that's why a prism splits sunlight into ROY G BIV. Each color is just a different wavelength of energy: red has the longest wavelength (~700nm) and violet the shortest (~400nm). When you mix colored light (not paint!), the colors add together — red + green = yellow, and all seven together approach white. This is called additive color mixing, and it's exactly how your phone screen works: millions of tiny red, green, and blue pixels blending to make every color you see.

SHOT HELPER
Real tips to improve your IRL game
BEEF — The Golden Formula
Balance your feet shoulder-width apart. Elbow tucked in under the ball, pointing at the basket. Eyes locked on the back of the rim — not the ball. Follow through by snapping your wrist down so your hand looks like it's reaching into a cookie jar on a high shelf. Every great shooter uses BEEF without thinking about it.
Arc Is Everything
A flat shot has almost no margin for error — it has to be nearly perfect to go in. A high arc shot can hit the back of the rim and still bounce in. Aim for about a 45-degree angle minimum. Think of shooting OVER the backboard, not AT the hoop. Stephen Curry shoots at 47 degrees average — that's why even his off-shots rattle in.
Use Your Legs, Not Just Your Arms
Most beginners push the ball with their arms and wonder why they get tired and inconsistent. Your legs are your power source. Bend your knees as you catch the ball, then extend your legs as you shoot — the energy flows up through your body into your wrist. This is why even small players can drain 3-pointers. Legs drive the distance, wrist drives the arc.
The Guide Hand Is Just a Guide
Your non-dominant hand (the one on the side of the ball) should never push the ball. It just holds it steady like a shelf. When you release, that hand should drop away naturally — if it's spinning the ball sideways or following through, it means you're using it to shoot, which kills accuracy. Practice shooting with just your dominant hand from close range to train this muscle memory.
Shoot the Same Way Every Time
Consistency beats power. Pick a spot on the rim to aim at (most pros aim at the back of the rim or just above it) and use the exact same motion every single shot. Your muscle memory needs hundreds of reps of the same motion to automate it. If you change your form every shot, your brain never builds the pattern. 50 focused shots beats 200 sloppy ones every time.
Don’t Watch the Ball — Watch the Rim
Your eyes should be on the rim before you even start your shooting motion, and they should stay there through the release. Don’t track the ball in the air. This forces your brain to compute the trajectory automatically (like catching a ball without thinking about physics). Kobe Bryant famously stared at the rim so intensely that defenders said it was unsettling.
Start Close, Then Move Back
If your form breaks down at range, you’re shooting from too far out. Start from 3 feet away and make 10 in a row with perfect form. Then step back to 6 feet, repeat. Work out to the 3-point line over weeks. Building form close up and expanding the range is how every shooting coach trains players from youth leagues all the way to the NBA.
